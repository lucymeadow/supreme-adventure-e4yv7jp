Hotels.com Homepage · Impact analysis

Boosting UK 10:100 loyalty messaging

  • ↑ Tripled 10:100 impressions
  • ↑ Doubled interactions with the 10:100 tile
  • ↓ 40-60% impression hit on the modules down the page
  • ↓ Pre/Post Estimate of $19m–$30m GBV cost over the 20-day boost

Study window 2026-04-30 → 2026-06-14. EGTnL 69824 ran UK at 100% from 2026-05-14 → 2026-06-02 and boosted the 10:100 merch tile to top of page. Summer Sale displaced it on 2026-06-03.

Prepared by Ice Peterman · HCOM Homepage Product Manager

The 2 things this paper answers

  1. What did the UK 10:100 merch tile boost do for the loyalty program, and did it have any impact on our other down-the-funnel metrics?
  2. If we want to repeat the boost after Summer Sale ends, what is the recommended path?

Context: the boost ran UK-only at 100% from 2026-05-14 to 2026-06-02 via feature toggle 69824. Summer Sale launched 2026-06-03 and we moved the merch tile back to its normal position. This summary uses clickstream data and is only a pre/post analysis. We need to run an A/B test to conclusively determine changes. The OneKey enrollment numbers are not in scope here.

What the data says

01

The boost was an impression win, not a CTR win.

Impressions tripled (87k → 231k/day) and clicks doubled (125 → 260/day), but CTR fell from 0.143% to 0.113%.

Estimated GBV impact

The boost cost roughly $19m–$30m in GBV over the 20-day window.

Hannah Chetwynd-Diggle's Home post-engagement dashboard values each module interaction at a specific GBV for Hcom UK: merch tile $58, RVP $137, RSD $119, FYB $264. Applying those rates to the interaction deltas between PRE and DURING gives a directional cost for the 10:100 boost.

PRE baseline: daily impressions, CTR, and clicks averaged over 2026-04-30 to 2026-05-13 (14 days). DURING: 2026-05-14 to 2026-06-02 (20 days). Two methods isolate different parts of the loss.

Method 1 — reach loss only

DURING impressions × PRE CTR. Holds engagement constant and asks: how many clicks would we have gotten at the PRE click-rate, given the lower DURING traffic?

−$19.3m GBV
across the 20-day boost ($965k/day)

Method 2 — full observed

Actual DURING clicks vs PRE-baseline clicks. Captures both the reach loss and the CTR drop on the surviving traffic (per-render for FYB, per-card for RVP/RSD as originally computed).

−$30.1m GBV
across the 20-day boost ($1.51m/day)

How to read the gap. Method 1 isolates the cost of pushing modules below the fold (reach loss only). Method 2 adds in the fact that DURING CTR also fell on FYB (−58% per-render), RVP (−34% per-session), and RSD (−49% per-session), so the surviving traffic also converted worse. The $10.9m gap between the methods is the CTR-degradation portion of the cost. Note on the denominator. The displacement section above uses per-session CTR for RVP and RSD to align with the Home Module Scorecards convention. The GBV math here uses raw per-card click counts (FYB complete_booking_*.selected, RVP recently_viewed_item.selected, RSD recent_search_item.selected) because the source dashboard's $/click rates attribute on a per-card-click basis. The CTR-denominator choice is a presentation/framing call for the displacement narrative; it does not change the underlying click counts, so the GBV totals above are unaffected.

The trade is roughly 80–120× underwater. The merch tile generated about $160k–$240k of incremental GBV during the boost. The modules pushed down the page lost $19.5m–$30.3m. Every $1 of GBV captured at top of page displaced $80–$120 of GBV from FYB, RVP, and RSD.

Caveats on these numbers. These estimates can only be proven through AB testing. The $/interaction rates are point-in-time post-engagement attributions from the UK Home dashboard, not causal lift estimates — they assume the average historical GBV per click is what the next missing click would have produced. The PRE baseline assumes interactions would have continued at the PRE rate absent the boost; CA shows no boost-shaped step on any module, so this is a defensible baseline but not a control. The merch tile gain holds it to top of page only (10:100-style boost); a different creative could perform differently. Numbers are quoted in $ to match the source dashboard.

Three options on the table for the next 10:100 boost, with trade-offs and a recommendation.

Ask 2 · Path forward

Three options for the next 10:100 boost.

If we want to re-run the boost after Summer Sale ends, here are three proposals.

Recommended
01

Single merch card

One merchandising card at top of page. No secondary card alongside it. Use a 50/50 A/B test to validate the experience.

Displacement: least impactful to the modules below it.

GBV cost: less than Option 2's $19m–$30m, with the removal of the second card.

Engineering cost: low — we need to make a copy of the existing merch tile UI, add it to the page, and connect it to experience manager.

02

Double merch card (May 2026 boost)

Two stacked merchandising cards at the top of page, 10:100 in the top slot. This is the exact configuration that ran 2026-05-14 to 2026-06-02. Use a 50/50 A/B test to directly measure the results.

Displacement: most impactful, of the options proposed, to the modules below it.

GBV cost: $19m–$30m over a 20-day boost.

Engineering cost: none, this already exists.

Not recommended without an A/B test
03

Hero banner

Uses the Sale Hero banner to promote 10:100 in brand colors. This is a larger canvas than the single merch tile. Use a 50/50 A/B test to validate the experience.

Displacement: more impactful than the single merch card to the modules below it.

GBV cost: untested. Cannot estimate without an A/B test — the visual treatment is novel and we have no analog in the historical data.

Engineering cost: medium — this card is tied to the slim search form experience within the summer sale. Parts of it need to be re-written to break it out for this use case.

Creative cost: we may require new assets as the existing 10:100 assets were created for merch cards.

Caveats

What this analysis cannot tell us.

Four things to hold lightly when reading the numbers above.

Methodology note. CTR denominators are module-specific. FYB and the 10:100 / RTB tile use per-impression CTR (count(.selected) / count(.presented)) because complete_booking.presented and the position-0 tile fire once per module render. RVP and RSD use per-session CTR (sessions-with-click / sessions-with-impression) because recently_viewed_item.presented and recent_search_item.presented fire per individual card or chip — a per-impression denominator would inflate to <1% and miss the canonical Home Module Scorecards rate. All metrics scoped by event_name and Home page only. We do not filter by event.event_category: that field was renamed mid-study during the Hotels.com Home rebrand on 2026-05-19/20, and re-pulls with the category filter silently zero out RVP DURING/POST volume.

No A/B test

The boost ran UK at 100%. All comparisons here are pre/post with a Canada sanity check. Audience-mix and seasonality confounds cannot be fully ruled out.

10:100 tile identified by position only

Tile content tokens were null on the merch tile rail in clickstream. The analysis uses component_position = 0 in DURING as the 10:100 tile proxy.

Home rebrand mid-study

On 2026-05-19/20, Hotels.com Home consolidated Finish Your Booking and Recently Viewed Properties into a new "Pick Up Where You Left Off" module. The events kept their original names (complete_booking.presented, recently_viewed_item.presented) but moved to a new event_category. Numbers in this doc are corrected to query by event_name + page only. Prior versions filtered by the old category and reported a false ~98% cliff that doesn't exist in the underlying event volume.

Confounds during the POST window

POST (2026-06-03 onward) includes Summer Sale, which changes both creative and ranking. CA POST also drops on FYB and RVP, so part of the POST signal is not UK-specific. POST vs DURING is the cleanest read for sale-carousel-vs-merch-tile displacement; POST vs PRE conflates the boost recovery with the sale.
