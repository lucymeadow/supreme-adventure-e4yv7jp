Price Risk Engine

A partnership opportunity: HCOM and InsurTech co-drive the platform that lets EG take calibrated travel-pricing risk onto its own balance sheet — and the two consumer products that ride it. Two HCOM customer problems converge on the same solution: travelers walking away from non-refundable rates because flexibility costs too much, and gold and platinum loyalty members shopping, booking, and staying without ever feeling their tier mattered. A shared ML pricing-and-risk engine solves both at scale by predicting when EG can profitably hold the risk on its own balance sheet — surfaced through EG-Funded Flex Rates (flex offers on otherwise non-refundable hotel rates) and Predictive Elite Upgrades (auto-confirmed room upgrades at booking for gold and platinum loyalty members).

05What this is

What we'd actually be building.

A shared engine that prices travel-risk decisions, plus two consumer products that ask it questions. Each piece has its own draft PRD.

Consumer Product · HCOM
EG-Funded Flex Rates
The customer-facing offer that changes how Hotels.com sells flexibility. It converts non-refundable rates into flexible offers priced by EG's own model, not by an insurer's margin stack.
How the engine feeds itThe Price Risk Engine predicts the future refundable price for the property, dates, and lead time. The flex offer surfaces only when the engine returns a profitable decision.
Consumer Product · HCOM
Predictive Elite Upgrades
The customer-facing product that makes loyalty status tangible. It auto-confirms a room upgrade for gold and platinum members at the moment of booking, instead of leaving it to whatever the hotel front desk has open at check-in.
How the engine feeds itThe Price Risk Engine models price convergence between room types. The upgrade auto-confirms only when the model predicts the premium room will trade near the base rate before the stay.
Both ask the engine: “Should we offer this, and at what price?”
Shared Platform · Co-driven
Price Risk Engine
The system that decides — and lets EG safely hold the risk on its own books.
ML prediction
Models future hotel pricing, resale probability, and room-type spread.
Risk engine
Returns an offer-or-decline decision per booking, with portfolio limits enforced.
Kill-switches
Suppress offers in <60 seconds if exposure or losses trip a threshold.
Captive integration
Settles the spread on EG's captive subsidiary's books with full audit trail.
How a flex transaction settles
When the Price Risk Engine predicts the future refundable price will fall at or below the current NR price plus a risk margin, EG surfaces the flex offer, remits the NR amount to the hotel, and absorbs the spread risk through its captive subsidiary — an EG-owned insurance entity that holds and manages the risk.
How an upgrade transaction settles
When the engine predicts a premium room will trade near the base rate before the stay, EG buys the base room, auto-confirms the upgrade at booking for an eligible loyalty member, and absorbs the spread risk through the same captive subsidiary.

A1The engine's three existential gates

All three are InsurTech-native problems.

The engine PRD is structured around three gates. Wrong call on any of them and the engine doesn't ship in that market.

Gate 01
Legal classification
Is engine output balance-sheet risk or insurance?
InsurTech already runs this analysis per jurisdiction for CFAR. Nobody else in EG knows this terrain. Wrong call here means the engine can't operate in that market.
Gate 02
Captive subsidiary GL & settlement
How does decision-to-money actually work?
The captive sits in InsurTech's perimeter. The engine is what makes it produce revenue, not just hold liability. Wave 1 spike defines feasibility.
Gate 03
Risk pricing models
Where does this DS/ML capability come from?
Extends InsurTech's existing CFAR DS/ML work (MLSTN1-3593). The engine is the next-gen InsurTech Pricing Service — they should converge.
The partnership logic

Each side brings something the other amplifies. HCOM brings the consumer surfaces, the merchandising muscle, and the CFAR program shipping H1 2026 — real demand the engine can ride from day one. InsurTech brings the captive subsidiary, the regulatory posture to operate it, and the actuarial capability to underwrite at portfolio scale. Co-driving turns this from a single HCOM product into EG's first risk-pricing platform.

A4Back-pocket answers

Likely follow-ups from Jonathan.

Click to expand. All open when printed.

Why HCOM and not InsurTech writing this PRD in the first place?+
Because the consumer pain (flex, loyalty perception) lives on HCOM and the merchandising surfaces are HCOM's. The capability (captive, pricing, legal posture) lives in InsurTech. Either org could have owned the engine on paper. The right answer is shared ownership, and we'd rather propose that now than fight about org charts later.
How does this relate to CFAR Wave 1?+
Complements, doesn't replace. CFAR is third-party insurance underwritten by Cover Genius — broad coverage by design. The engine takes balance-sheet risk on a high-confidence subset. Portfolio of two products on a shared merchandising surface, deconflicted by the Flex Rates PRD.
What's the regulatory risk?+
Legal classification is the existential gate. Wave 1 deliverable. If Legal classifies engine output as insurance in a target market, the engine doesn't operate there. That's a hard fail in the PRD, not a sticky note. We sequenced it first deliberately so we don't build a model and a captive integration that then can't ship.
What's the captive subsidiary actually ready for?+
Entity is stood up. GL integration is the Wave 1 spike — throwaway code that records a simulated offer-to-settlement flow end-to-end. Production-grade GL flows land in Wave 3. We're not pretending the captive is ready today; we're naming the work to make it ready.
What's the size of the prize?+
On flex: target >3% attach in 90 days, >8% steady-state, vs Booking's Smart Flex at 0.02-0.79% — the gap comes from selectivity, since the engine only surfaces an offer on high-confidence predictions. Net cost per room night targets <$2.50 in year one. CVR lift is deliberately left as a backtest-sized range with sensitivity bounds rather than a stated number — no validated industry benchmark anchors a magnitude, and a target we can't ground is worse than no target. The engine-economics floor is "outperform insured-flex on the eligible cohort." On the engine: each new consumer offer onboards in weeks once the platform exists, instead of standing up from zero. The dollar figure on incremental NBV is what the Wave 1 backtest produces. We don't pretend to know it yet, and we shouldn't.
Why two consumer products on day one? Isn't that scope creep?+
The opposite. One consumer product doesn't justify a platform investment. Two does, because it forces the decision interface and multi-tenancy to be real, not theoretical. And the second product (upgrades) is on a different demand surface with different unit economics — if both work, the platform thesis is proven and the next consumer offer is cheap.
What if the model can't hit 75% precision on resale?+
The engine doesn't enter Wave 2. Flex doesn't ship in that market. That's the kill threshold. We'd either widen data, redesign features, or scope down geography. The PRD has the pivot triggers written down.
Who's on the hook if a loss spike happens?+
Central Risk on-call has unilateral kill-switch authority by POS, Point of Supply, partner, or lead-time band. No deploy, no approval queue, <60-second propagation. That's a hard Wave 3 requirement. Naming the on-call owner is part of the Wave 1 RACI fill.
What's the difference between this and Hopper?+
Hopper takes price risk on flights mostly, funded by accuracy. We're taking room-pricing and room-type risk on hotels, funded by EG's captive instead of Hopper's venture capital. Same logic, different asset class — and we have the merchant inventory they don't.
What does HCOM commit to if InsurTech says yes?+
Consumer-product PMs and engineers on both Flex Rates and Predictive Upgrades. The discovery work (manual demand probes, copy tests, partner reaction tests). Merchandising surfaces. Partner Central opt-out controls. The captive funding plan from the HCOM P&L view.
What does InsurTech commit to if you say yes?+
Captive operations and GL integration ownership. Central-Risk staffing for the new asset class VaR work. Legal classification track lead. Actuarial capability check. DS/ML pairing on the engine team.
Why now?+
Three exogenous events converged in the last 12 months. CFAR pilot is shipping H1 2026 — InsurTech has the operational muscle. The captive subsidiary was recently created — the legal entity exists. Booking's Smart Flex badging change in Sept 2025 made flexibility a visible competitive surface for the first time. The wedge is open. If we wait a year, Booking or Hopper closes it.
