The resourcing gap I filled

Hotels.com presents vacation rentals in a way that's visually indistinguishable from hotels. VR is only 3.8% of orders but generates a disproportionate share of customer confusion, price surprises, and mistrust. The Differentiated VR PRD laid out a five-wave fix, starting with clarity: clearly label non-hotel inventory before the click.

Wave 1 was ready to learn from — but there was no engineering team allocated to it, and the design partner's time was limited. The work could have sat in a backlog waiting for a quarter to free up capacity.

Instead, I treated it as a Builder problem: confirm I had the access, learn the systems, and ship a real Test-and-Learn myself. That meant being the PM, the designer, and the developer — one person carrying a change from a strategy document all the way into production traffic on Hotels.com.

01

Three job descriptions, one builder

The product development lifecycle usually splits across a PM, a designer, and one or more engineers. On this Test-and-Learn, I held all three — and the AI-empowered workflow made that leverage possible.

Product Manager

Framed the problem and the experiment

  • Authored the Differentiated VR PRD — five delivery waves, target users, functional requirements, and guardrails
  • Resolved all six open questions into documented decisions (visual approach, VR visibility, data thresholds, naming)
  • Scoped the Wave 1 Test-and-Learn and registered the EGTnL experiment — hypothesis, metrics, entity, and sizing
  • Ran an analytics peer review with HCOM Analytics and rebuilt the metric set to a do-no-harm frame
  • Sequenced the TnL ahead of the differentiation epic and owned the go-live decision
Product Designer

Set the pattern and the language

  • Wrote the Wave 1 Design Brief — design principles, conceptual model, and four end-to-end user flows
  • Chose a lightweight pill label over a card redesign: identify before the click, within the existing layout
  • Aligned the naming decision (Entire {type} → Vacation rental) with XD design partner Janice Yeung
  • Caught and fixed a localization casing defect (sentence case per EG guidelines) before translations fired
  • Built the interactive VR differentiation prototype and staged Figma control/treatment visuals
Software Engineer

Wrote, tested, and shipped the code

  • Traced the SRP label across three repos and found the real source: server-side in EALS (Kotlin)
  • Pivoted from a client-side approach to a server-driven implementation after architecture review
  • Wrote the experiment-gated adapter + a 13-type property allowlist, with Cypress + Jest test coverage
  • Shipped three merged PRs (feature + en_US + non-en_US localization) through CODEOWNERS review and the merge queue
  • Verified end-to-end with Playwright probes, an independent clean-room agent, and a production canary
02

What actually shipped

A production Test-and-Learn that swaps the SRP card descriptor for vacation-rental property types, gated behind an experiment flag and localized for every point of sale.

The change lives server-side in experience-api-lodging-search, the source of truth for the search results card. A new VacationRentalPropertyTypeOverrideExperiment helper and a 13-type structure allowlist decide when to override the descriptor — so hotels, resorts, and aparthotels are never mislabeled.

It shipped as three merged PRs: the feature, the en_US string, and BabelPrime translations across 30+ locale bundles (UK English correctly reads Holiday rental, German Ferienunterkunft).

Brand safety moved off-code into the experiment's targeting rules, matching the EG-preferred pattern — so the same adapter is safe for every brand that consumes EALS.

The readout

The experiment was framed as a do-no-harm guardrail test: prove the clearer label doesn't hurt conversion before the wider epic invests in it. It did exactly that.
